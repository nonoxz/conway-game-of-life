Core idea

What is it about?

The Game of Life has no objective, score, or opponents. It is a zero-player game: once the initial state is set, each generation is calculated automatically from neighboring cells.

  1. A live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies by isolation.
  2. A live cell with two or three live neighbors survives.
  3. A live cell with more than three live neighbors dies by overcrowding.
  4. A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive.

Historical context

History of the Game of Life

1940s

Von Neumann's question

John von Neumann studied machines capable of self-replication. Conway looked for a much simpler version of those ideas using local rules.

1970

Conway and Gardner

John Horton Conway introduced Life, and Martin Gardner popularized it in Scientific American, turning it into a classic of recreational mathematics.

1970 onward

Gliders, guns, and computation

Bill Gosper discovered the first glider gun, proving unbounded growth. Later work showed that Life can implement universal computation.

Today

A living community

Researchers and enthusiasts continue to find oscillators, spaceships, giant patterns, and formal proofs about this minimal universe.
